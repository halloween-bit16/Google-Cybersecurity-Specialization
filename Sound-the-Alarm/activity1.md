# Incident Handler's Journal

---

## Journal Entry #1

**Date:** June 18, 2026

**Description:**
Documentation of a ransomware attack on a small U.S. healthcare clinic. The incident was initiated via a phishing email containing a malicious attachment, which when downloaded, deployed ransomware that encrypted critical files and disrupted all business operations.

**Tool(s) Used:** None at this stage — initial documentation and incident recording only.

---

## The 5 W's

**Who caused the incident?**
An organized group of unethical hackers with a known history of targeting organizations in the healthcare and transportation industries caused the incident. The group deliberately selected this clinic as their target and orchestrated the phishing campaign.

**What happened?**
Employees received targeted phishing emails containing malicious attachments. Once an employee downloaded the attachment, malware was installed on their computer, which then deployed ransomware that encrypted all critical company files. A ransom note was displayed on affected computers demanding a large sum of money in exchange for the decryption key.

**When did the incident occur?**
The incident occurred on a Tuesday morning at approximately 9:00 a.m. Employees discovered they were unable to access their files and computers at this time, triggering the incident response process.

**Where did the incident happen?**
The incident occurred at a small U.S. health care clinic specializing in primary-care services. The attack impacted the clinic's internal computer network and systems, including access to critical patient medical records and operational software.

**Why did the incident happen?**
The incident happened because employees were successfully deceived by targeted phishing emails (spear phishing). The organization lacked sufficient technical controls to detect and block the malicious email attachments before they were downloaded, allowing the malware to execute and spread ransomware across the network.

---

## Additional Notes

- It is unclear how many employees clicked the malicious attachment — determining the exact initial entry point(s) will be critical during the investigation phase.
- The clinic was forced to shut down all computer systems, raising questions about whether a proper incident response plan and data backup strategy were in place prior to the attack.
- Key question: Did the organization pay the ransom, and if so, were the files successfully decrypted? Paying ransoms is generally discouraged as it does not guarantee recovery and incentivizes further attacks.
- This incident highlights the urgent need for employee phishing awareness training and endpoint protection software across all workstations.
